LinkedIn Data Breach

Verified Breach June 2012

164 million user accounts were compromised in this major professional network breach

Official Response

What Happened

In June 2012, LinkedIn suffered a major data breach that exposed the stored credentials of 164 million users. The passwords were stored as SHA1 hashes without salt, making them easily crackable. The data remained out of sight until being offered for sale on a dark market site 4 years later in 2016.

The breach was initially thought to include only 6.5 million accounts, but the full extent was revealed years later when the complete dataset appeared for sale on the dark web. LinkedIn confirmed the additional exposure and forced password resets for all accounts that hadn't changed their passwords since 2012.

This breach highlighted the importance of proper password storage techniques and the need for companies to implement stronger security measures like salting and using more robust hashing algorithms.

Recommended Actions

  • Change Your Password

    If you haven't changed your LinkedIn password since 2012, do so immediately.

  • Enable Two-Factor Authentication

    Add an extra layer of security to your LinkedIn account.

  • Check Other Accounts

    If you used the same password elsewhere, change those too.

  • Monitor for Suspicious Activity

    Watch for unusual login attempts or messages from your account.
